World Hemophilia Day 2015 : Building a Family of Support

  • May 13 2015
  • Letter to HASL

World Hemophilia Day is marked each year globally to help increase awareness of hemophilia and other inherited bleeding disorders. This is especially necessary for the global bleeding disorders community as many still remain without diagnosis or treatment.

Raising awareness often starts with those closest to people with hemophilia. World Hemophilia Day provides an opportunity to reach out and communicate with immediate and extended family, with friends, and with colleagues. These support networks play an integral role in patientsâ€™ lives, as they have the capabilities to encourage and advocate for people with bleeding disorders. On this World Hemophilia Day, we are encouraging the global community to come together to recognize the importance of building a family of support.

Surrounding people with hemophilia with this type of encouragement is especially necessary for those facing a situation of inadequate availability of care or no access to treatment at all. We live in an era of rapidly improving effective and life-altering medications and care, exponentially increasing with each passing year.

Yet at the same time, the gap between those with access to these treatments also greatly increases. More often than not, children who grow up with a bleeding disorder suffer needlessly. This in turn translates in significant decreases in the quality of life, with many missing school or not attending at all. The reality is that it is indeed possible for those living with hemophilia to experience an improved quality of life. To be able to work, to raise a family, and to contribute to their own society is attainable. This is only possible with a concerted effort by the entire community, both locally and at a global level.

The WFH also supports the work of its national member organizations, including the Hemophilia Association of Sri Lanka (HASL), in their mission to advance care within their own communities. In 2013 only 778people with hemophilia were identified in Sri Lanka. The need for increased diagnosis, effective training, and strengthened support networks remain a priority.

The WFH would like to congratulate the HASL on the launch of their newsletter and website which will help them continue to raise awareness. We also pledge our continued support to your community, HASL, the medical community in Sri Lanka, the College of Haematologists, and the Ministry of Health, as you all work together advancing the mission of improving and sustaining care for all people in Sri Lanka with inherited bleeding disorders.

Sincerely,

Alain Weill
